Introduction

Abstract

The contemporary Russian art community is under the constant watchful eyes of conservative activist groups and the government, and self-censorship is the norm even though the government claims that it does not interfere in this domain. What happened? Pussy Riot’s 2012 performance and trial were a turning point in the development of the Russian art world and catalyzed the implementation of new mechanisms of control. The question at the heart of this study is: how has the Russian government’s policy, from 2012 onwards, influenced artistic life in Russia and contributed to artists’ perceived need to self-censor their work? To unpack what changed and why, this thesis examines the changes in the relationship between the Russian government and artists as well as the methods used by the state to exert influence over artists, and finds linkages between contemporary censorship policy and censorship in Stalin’s USSR. In addition, the thesis draws from the author’s personal experiences with self-censorship as well as those of other Russian artists in order to capture this phenomenon’s mechanisms at both a macro and micro level.

Introduction

“To censor or destroy a work is to testify to its hold over its public. It is to acknowledge the work’s seductiveness (whether sexual or political or both) and to admit that what should not have power actually does.” (Freedberg, 2016, p. 68)

When we think of censorship, we typically imagine a register of concrete rules and prohibitions, but in the contemporary world, where access to increasingly abundant sources of information is similarly ever growing, there are few countries that can afford such traditional mass censorship campaigns. Usually, these are very closed states where there is a high degree of control over different forms of self-expression. Contemporary authoritarian regimes have abandoned this scheme for the most part and, instead, seek to introduce systems of pressure whereby society begins to censor itself. This type of censorship is applied selectively, is less expensive, maintains a semblance of freedom, and the public typically scrutinizes the ‘propriety’ of art instead of the state.

In September 2016, a scandal erupted in the Russian art world. The American photographer Jock Sturges aroused indignation among conservatives in Russian society, because there were rumors that photographs of naked children were on display (Igritsky, 2016). In response, a group of well-organized activists calling themselves the ‘Officers of Russia’ blocked access to the gallery at which the artist’s exhibition was held (Antonova, 2017). This event was important not because people assembled for their collective aversion to photos of nude underage models (even though this later proved to be false), but because a group of people were able to get a gallery to voluntarily close an exhibition in response to claims that it was eroding society’s morality (Igritsky, 2016). Sturges’ exhibition was closed purely because of pressure by the ‘Officers of Russia’, not because the work on display somehow violated any laws as this had been reviewed prior to its opening (Igritsky, 2016; Antonova, 2017). This shows that any organization with sufficient human and financial resources, and whose mission statement is to defend Russian-Orthodoxy or patriotism, can censor artistic life in the country without any contestation by the government.

This case is illustrative of what I would like to talk about in my thesis, namely how the Russian government’s policies have normalized aggression against artists and created an atmosphere of self-censorship in the Russian art world. While there is no official censorship policy with respect to art in the country, the Russian government has sought to exert control over the art community in more indirect ways. As will be made evident over the course of this thesis, the government’s actions indicate that it views artists who are critical of the socio-economic conditions of the country, its institutions, history, or leadership as its political opponents. It has been officially declared that the art community and society should filter undesirable elements out independently, without interference from the government (“Putin about Censorship,” 2017); yet, the government has only been (financially) supporting art of a nature and content that is in tune with its own ideals (Medinsky, 2015) and expectations of art. This so-called filtering mechanism has effectively amounted to a coercive form of self-censorship imposed upon the producers of any content that is perceived to conflict with conservative, traditional, religious, or patriotic values. For these reasons, Russia serves as a compelling case to examine contemporary types of censorship and how self-censorship takes root in society.

In the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics (USSR), authorities defined a rigid censorship policy and warned that it was forbidden to go beyond what was stipulated. The functional act of censoring content was entrusted to special state institutions. Artists and writers usually knew how to behave and could make judgments as to how far they were willing to go in their work. In contrast, an important aspect of contemporary repression of art and artists in Russia is its inconsistency and selectivity. Opponents of the regime are still more or less free in their statements and media remain relatively independent, too. However, certain laws are formulated in such a way that it is unclear what is forbidden and what is not. For one and the same activity, some people are punished while others are not. This has created a gray zone of uncertainty around controversial subjects, which has had a far larger scope as a discursive deterrent than a direct prohibition of specific subject matter. Everything that is inside or even approaching this gray zone could be perceived as filterable material. Fearing the consequences of violating unclear rules, self-censorship sets in. Another noteworthy component of contemporary censorship in Russia is that pro-government and pro-Church organizations have assumed the role of censors. These organizations, emboldened and legitimated by loosely-formulated laws that limit expression, take it upon themselves to decide what is right and wrong under the aegis of the defending morality in society.

It is important to research what is happening in Russia to understand how new forms of control are emerging, because, like in Russia, much of Western society has recently seen a surge in the kind of conservatism, fanatical patriotism, and political polarization that gave birth to contemporary Russian censorship. The loss of socially-critical art closes one of the engines of self-criticism in society, furthering the creation of an ideological echo-chamber. As the space for the free expression of ideas narrows, even the most innocuous subjects become politicized and the degree of societal aggression grows. Furthermore, self-censorship in fear of reprisals erodes the freedom of expression in society, and suppression of criticism or debate undermines a fundamental (and often constitutional) agreement between the citizens of the country and their government on which the idea of democracy relies. In a democracy, what is the government if not an extension of the will of its people? Yet, in the absence of any official censorship, the illusion of a fully democratic society is sustained.

As a Russian artist, the state of the Russian art world affects me one way or another. Whether I return to Russia or stay in Europe changes little about the possibility of my work as an artist becoming a problem for my family. In addition to this, the events taking place in my home country have an effect on my relationship to the themes I want to work with. I constantly ask myself if I would have written this thesis or made a project about censorship if still lived in Russia. Writing this thesis has been a way of making the phenomenon of self-censorship more understandable for myself, both at a personal level and as an artist, allowing me to recognize it in myself, cope with it, and understand how it influences my work. Furthermore, it made it possible for me to situate a long chain of incidents within a broader current of developments that have had deep and lasting ramifications for the art world of my home country.

Aggression against artists in Russia has gradually intensified over the past years, but because each individual amplification was relatively small it was not until much later that it became apparent that circumstances had changed. Behavior that would have seemed impossible less than a decade ago has been normalized. Hence, one of the aims of this thesis is to provide a reference point so that the gravity of the contemporary context is made evident. To adequately reflect on how contemporary censorship in Russia operates and what its effects have been, this thesis seeks to answer the following research question: how has the Russian government’s policy, from 2012 onwards, influenced artistic life in Russia and contributed to artists’ perceived need to self-censor their work? In order to research how self-censorship has taken root in the present context, this thesis looks at various aspects underlying this phenomenon and draws parallels between historical and contemporary events. Although the main time period under analysis of 2012 to 2017 is relatively short, there have been an abundance of cases and precedents set on how artists are to conduct themselves. Building upon the understanding gained about the roots and mechanisms of self-censorship within the Russian art society, the thesis also unpacks how self-censorship is elicited at the individual level on the basis of personal experiences and insights from other Russian artists.

The first chapter provides a foundation for the central concepts of this thesis: censorship and self-censorship. Drawing from academic literature and other secondary sources, it clarifies the distinctions between traditional censorship and its contemporary equivalent. Furthermore, it defines the concept of self-censorship and explains the mechanisms that make it such an effective tool for curtailing undesirable behavior in society.

Next, the second chapter of the thesis provides a brief historical review of Russian visual art from 1900 to 1954 based on secondary literature on this time period. Direct prohibitions and repression after the 1930s led to the institutionalization of the main artistic direction during Stalin's reign: socialist realism. It served as a medium for the spread of propaganda. This section focuses on how the censorship of art worked as well as how it affected the development of visual art at the time, and it serves as a constant anchor for the relevance of policies and events in contemporary Russia.

Following this, the third chapter examines the Pussy Riot scandal of 2012, the political atmosphere at the time, the adaptation of a law on the protection of the religious community’s feelings that legitimized much of the aggression against artists since 2012, and how all these events eventually led to the emergence of the kind of pressure groups that, today, are highly influential agents in the realm of censorship in Russia. This will rely on secondary literature on the Pussy Riot case, accounts from members of the group, as well as numerous Russian news reports on these events.

Subsequently, the thesis’s fourth chapter explores important cases that took place between 2012 and 2017 in which pro-government and pro-Church pressure groups were able shut down exhibitions, plays, and movies in Russia. It also looks at a variety of important events, public statements, and precedents that shed light on the contemporary relationship between artists and the state. The aim of this is to show how, over time, this entrenched the understanding among artists that touching upon certain subjects in their work will evoke hostile reactions from society, thereby creating a perceived necessity to self-censor one’s work. To do so, this section draws on the personal accounts of various artists, statements from Russian policy-makers, news reports, as well as secondary literature on censorship in the art world.

Having addressed the historical and political foundations of the contemporary state of Russia’s art world as well as how the need for self-censorship among artists arose, the fifth and final chapter goes into my personal experiences of and motivations to self-censor, as well as the influence of self-censorship on artists at an individual level. This phenomenon is more than a defense mechanism against chastisement by the government or society; underlying the decision to censor your thoughts and work as an artist is a complex and often confusing confluence of motivations. Included are also insights on the subject from Russian artists who also live and work in Russia at the moment.

Chapter 1

The Concepts of Censorship
and Self-Censorship

Before proceeding to the body of the thesis, it is worth discussing the basic concepts of censorship and self-censorship that are at the heart of this research and elaborate how these are understood in the context of this thesis. Historically, in countries such as the USSR, censorship operated as a bureaucratic system of rule enforcement, which forbade the publication of specific books or topics and even maintained lists of specific phrases that were prohibited to use (Snegovaya, 2015).

In today’s world, this ‘traditional’ approach is not feasible or practical due developments such as the Internet, the access to which has radically expanded the scope of information and content to be policed and monitored (Walker & Orttung, 2014, p. 77). To continue to stifle undesirable material and knowledge within this new paradigm, censorship strategies needed to adapt. Walker and Orttung (2014) succinctly describe the distinction between these two eras of censorship as follows: “old-school totalitarians were do-it-yourselfers; modern authoritarians like to outsource and, where possible, use market forces to enhance censorship capacity” (p. 79). Because economic growth and development demand access to information and its exchange, the heavy-handedness of old model of censorship would suffocate the possibility of succeeding in this (p. 72).

The goal of contemporary forms of censorship is to create a self-censored society that will regulate itself (Hassid, 2008, p. 421). Lee (1998) defines self-censorship in the context of news media as “a set of editorial actions ranging from omission, dilution, distortion, and change of emphasis to choice of rhetorical devices by journalists, their organizations, and even the entire media community in anticipation of currying reward and avoiding punishments from the power structure” (p. 57). As will be seen in the following chapters, these very same principles can be identified in and transposed onto the functioning of the Russian art sphere. Replacing the terms ‘journalists’ by ‘artists’ and ‘media community’ by ‘art community’ produces an operational description of self-censorship that largely captures how it functions in this thesis’s area of study.

Other authors describe the phenomenon differently. Groys (2016) and Mintcheva (2016) both point to fear as the primary catalyst for self-censorship, adding that fear can be further subdivided in fears of various outcomes. Another important dimension is context, as the choice (or the lack thereof) to self-censor is often a matter of “what can be shown where” (Mintcheva, 2016, p. 209). Moreover, whereas censorship often tends to be interpreted relatively unambiguously, self-censorship is characterized as “a complex picture of multiple considerations, contextual factors, and decisions often made behind the scenes” (p. 209). Distinguishing between the reasons for self-censorship in repressive societies and democratic ones, Mintcheva (2016) also asserts that in the former “self-censorship is the interiorized fear of government intervention,” whereas in the latter financial security is often the overriding factor, because “as public support shrinks, art institutions are dependent, for their very existence, on the whims of donors” (p. 208).

Lee’s (1998) definition of self-censorship captures the phenomenon as it works in the Russian art world relatively well. However, its scope is limited to the interactions between the art community and the government. In Russia, activist organization that claim to act on behalf of society also play an important role in driving self-censorship in the art community. Groys’s (2016) and Mintcheva’s (2016) descriptions are useful as well but are similarly incomplete if used as operational definitions. For this reason, elements of all these descriptions were synthesized to try to approximate a more fitting portrayal of the phenomenon. Self-censorship is taken to comprise the actions that individuals, organizations, or a community take to curb or somehow compromise expression in response to an internalized fear of potential punishment, abuse, or financial loss; and in order to earn the approval of their peers, the public, or authorities.

To create a self-censored society, governments have two important tools at their disposal: the selective punishment of undesirable agents in society on the basis of opaque rules to provoke uncertainty, and the use of financial dependence and incentives (Hassid, 2008, pp. 418, 422). Selectively rather than uniformly applying censorship is when for one and the same offense, certain people or organizations are reprimanded whereas others are not (Snegovaya, 2015). By combining punishment with its selective application and uncertainty in the scope of the rule that legitimates the punishment, its effectiveness as a deterrent is augmented, because this systematic uncertainty causes people to exercise greater caution in their actions than they would otherwise (Hassid, 2008, pp. 421, 425; Snegovaya, 2015). If it is uncertain where the line between appropriate and inappropriate is drawn, people will tend to be extra careful and distance themselves from where this juncture is approximated to be, rather than try to approach it as closely as possible without crossing it. In addition to this, the element of uncertainty offers the government significant flexibility in who it can target (Hassid, 2008, p. 421). Once uncertainty is sufficiently internalized and is anticipated by the members of the community subjected to self-censorship, the process that compels people to censor themselves becomes almost automatic, requiring minimal further input by authorities (p. 425).

Another method of generating self-censorship is through financial control. In the literature on news media self-censorship, this is known as the “marketization” of news (Hassid, 2008, p. 418). Marketization forces organizations or individuals that heavily rely on government subsidies or grants to acquiesce to the eligibility criteria of these to ensure organizational survival or commercial success. Similarly, even if organizations or artists do not rely on government funding per se, by linking the eligibility of public funds to specific expectations in terms of contents, themes, or style, the government artificially creates and sustains a market for this type of art. By tying funding and, by extension, visibility to strict criteria, certain forms of art become more popular to the exclusion of any material deemed undesirable (p. 418). Evidence of this having taken place in Russia is discussed in Chapter 4 of this thesis.

The use of the concepts of censorship and self-censorship in the following chapters will be based on the way they have been interpreted here.

Chapter 2

Socialist Realism

This chapter is about how the principal style of art in the Soviet Union came to be socialist realism, the primary goal of which was to serve the government’s interests and ideals (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-b). It seems obvious, perhaps, that in this totalitarian society art and artists were tasked with serving the goals of the state’s propaganda campaign. The art sphere was kept under strict and heavy-handed supervision; any disobedience was severely punished (Segal, 2016, p. 64). The goal of this chapter is to show how the relationship between artists and the government was composed and what principles it was based on under this totalitarian regime. This will later also help draw similarities between the government’s relationship toward art in Stalin’s time and in contemporary Russia.

From 1910 up to 1954, important changes took place in the (pre-) Soviet art sphere that influenced the relationship toward art in contemporary Russia in many ways (Kruglikov, 2013). In the place of the complex and highly diverse work of the Russian avant-garde came socialist realism. The core principles of this new concept were that it should be understandable to the general public and that it conformed to the ideals of the country’s new political life (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-b). In an interview with Clara Zetkin, Lenin expressed this idea as follows: “the socialist artist should make art for the working people, in a style accessible to all, that would inspire the realization of socialist society” (Segal, 2016, p. 63). Socialist realism encompassed all types of art in the USSR until the early 1960s and can be encountered in Russia even today (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-a).

Between 1914 and 1922, the Russian avant-garde flourished. This was a time of revolutions and civil war, when there was complete freedom of self-expression, and each artist could, in their own way, contemplate the world around them. The Russian avant-garde was made up of a variety of directions and concepts. Altogether, these shaped the art sphere at the time. Artists created their own schools and groups, wrote manifests, and each tried to emphasize the novelty and uniqueness of their work (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-a). Painting, literature, and music thrived in this period, which is also known as the silver age of Russian culture. In the first years following the 1917 revolution everything was as it had been previously — artists held exhibitions, went from one movement to another, and worked actively. However, following the 1922 civil war, a new and quite involved actor appeared amid them in the art world: the government (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-a).

Contour of Sportsmen by Kazimir Malevich. 1928-1932

Following a decade in which they were free from censorship and boundaries, artists found this development extremely surprising (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-a). The new government had begun to exercise control over all aspects of the art world. Initially, this entailed the purchase of artwork, holding exhibitions, and patronage (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-a). The government would buy artwork that reflected both its own as well as the general public’s tastes and of which the message was equally simple and understandable by all—any ideological messages had to be easily identifiable (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-b). The aesthetic style that developed from this was the foundation for socialist realism and would become a condition rather than a choice (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-b).

The Soviet regime sought to homogenize all artists, because this allowed them to control art (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-a). It is more difficult to control that which is constantly in motion and changing, as in the avant-garde period (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-a). Reflecting on the influence of art in its different forms (painting, cinema, literature, photography) over people, Stalin asserted that the artist is the human soul’s engineer (Segal, 2016, p. 65). By the end of the 1920s, the government had expanded its control over art even further by enacting direct censorship. One of the minds behind the Soviet system of censorship, who was also one of the program’s supervisors, wrote in 1928 that criticism must have consequences, namely arrests, judicial reprisals, harsh sentences, physical and moral executions (Ermakov, 2003).

Stalin and Voroshilov in the Kremlin by Aleksandr Gerasimov. 1938

After Stalin came to power, all cultural and political organizations were placed under tight supervision (Segal, 2016, p. 64). Over the course of 1937 to 1938—the peak of the Great Terror — it was demonstrated that any opposition to the regime (whether it was real or imagined) would not be tolerated (p. 64). In 1932, all art movements and groups were closed and forbidden by decree. In their place, the government-controlled Artists’ Union of the USSR was established (Segal, 2016, p. 64). From this point onward, the government became the sole patron in the art sphere (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-b). In the same year, in the pages of the newspaper Literaturnaya Gazeta, the concept of socialist realism was presented to the general public (Segal, 2016, p. 64). This concept had been discussed many times by artists and critics over the years prior (Dobrenko, 2007). Segal (2016) explains that “socialist art had to be dialectic; it should not depict the world as it is, in all its contingence and imperfection, but as it would reveal itself in all its splendor when the socialist utopia had been realized” (pp. 64-65).

Censorship, at this time, developed in its traditional sense. In order to secure control, the government primarily made use of fear and its ability to regulate who was allowed to work. (Segal, 2016, p. 64). It was not possible to find work if one was not a member of an official artists’ union. Not only were artists unable to organize exhibitions of their work independently, but they would simply be unable to acquire the materials needed to perform their work (p. 64). So, to be excluded from a union effectively meant being unemployed. In addition to this, an overseeing institution decided, among others, which paintings would be shown at an exhibition and whether a certain artist deserved exposure or punishment (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-b). Censors and critics were a part of this institution too. They had a great deal of influence over artists’ fate by deciding whose work would be published and whose would not be (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-b). It is not for nothing that in Bulgakov’s novel The Master and Margarita the first thing that Margarita did after her transformation into a witch was demolish the apartment of the critic who ruined the Master’s career.

One of the most commonly-used instruments of censorship was the law on wittingly spreading false fabrications for the defamation of the Soviet system (Art. 69 & Art. 70). Almost anything could be qualified to fall under this statute, even things as innocuous as misspellings (Vinokurov, P. 1937). An oversight by a critic or censor could cost them their life, as could permitting work that the government might find objectionable (“Fully expose bourgeois,” 1949).

Criticism of Stalin was out of the question. In November 1933, Osip Mandelstam wrote a poem in which he criticized Stalin, which (future Nobel Prize laureate) Boris Pasternak considered an act of suicide (Sarnov, 2003). In the night of May 13, 1934, the poet was arrested, and on December 27, 1938, Mandelstam passed away from heart failure in a Soviet labor camp at an unknown location (Sarnov, 2003; “Osip Mendelstam. 1891–1938,” n.d.). Thus, an artist’s main fear was, of course, not becoming unemployed but ending up in a labor camp or simply being executed.

Between 1937 and 1938, at least 1,344,923 people were imprisoned on charges of anti-Soviet activity, of whom 681,692 were sentenced to capital punishment (Zemskov, 1995, p. 123). Based on these figures, there would have been, on average, up to a thousand executions per day on the basis of this law across the Soviet Union.

The Stakhanovites by Aleksandr Deyneka. 1937

In a way, communist ideologues and bureaucrats acted as curators who used artists as vehicles for their artistic ideas and will (Kruglikov, 2013). The canon of socialist realism demanded of Soviet artists and writers that they depict only that what the government wished to see (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-b). There was a singular idea that stood behind all creative work: socialism’s victory and the accomplishment of a magnificent future. The subject matter was to be either contemporary or based on historical events that led to the splendor of contemporary Soviet society (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-b). The central goal of socialist realism was to convince its viewers of the righteousness of the USSR’s political and economic system (Segal, 2016, p. 61). Reality was replaced by an imagined representation of it, one that was more romantic and heroic and became propaganda for the economic and political system (Dobrenko, 2007). This was supposed to still represent reality, just a more glorified version of it (Dobrenko, 2007). Most importantly, people had to be able to identify with those heroes.

Portrait of Joseph Stalin by Pavel Filonov. 1936

According to Kruglikov (2013), the relationship between socialist realism and the USSR’s authorities is akin to the relationship between a religion and a deity, whereby the government took upon itself the role of such a deity, who was personified by Stalin. Anything that so much as suggested the denunciation of the government’s divinity, or was critical of it, would result in punishment. In the best case, someone would be exiled from society; in the worst case, they would be executed (Kruglikov, 2013). At its prime, socialist realism was deeply religious art and Stalin’s depiction in it often bore resemblance to religious icons (Kruglikov, 2013).

The state perceived culture exclusively as an instrument for agitation and propaganda. It spoke of the wonderful present and promised a magnificent future (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-b). Any criticism or pessimistic view of reality was vigorously suppressed. There was only space for praise, patriotism, and happiness (Gerasimov, 1949). Socialist realism was formed as a product of the totalitarianism of the USSR; its peak was under Stalin’s reign and his mass repressions. It was only after Stalin's death in 1953 and the concurrent abatement of the repressive climate that new art styles began to appear in the Soviet Union. Underground art and samizdat, the private reproduction of censored material, started emerging and artists were able to work outside of government-sanctioned channels (Yelshevskaya, n.d.-c).

Prison photo of Osip Mandelstam. 1934

To summarize, the relationship between artists and the government of the USSR developed gradually and followed a common pattern: control over artists’ activities, their financial dependence on the government, the inability to work without governmental unionization, total dependence on governmental orders of specific kinds of artwork, and a fear of being punished for deviating from established rules. The raison d’être of art in the USSR was to serve the interests of the government by creating propagandistic patriotic work that was intellectually accessible (Segal, 2016, p. 63).

In present-day Russia, the government’s relationship toward art bears some similarities to the time of socialist realism. The Russian minister of culture has, quite sincerely, stated that if the government hands out money to artists, it has the right to expect that the resultant work will be free of criticism in their direction and be patriotic (Medinsky, 2015). This relationship will be examined further in the next chapter.

Chapter 3

Pussy Riot

This chapter focuses on the Pussy Riot trial. This trial and the performance preceding it became turning points in the relationship between Russian artists and authorities. Following the case against the art group, the degree of aggression in society against artists reached all-new highs, which, over time, was normalized by the constant attacks on plays, exhibitions, and performances by pro-Church and pro-government activist groups. The government skillfully capitalized on these events in order to implement a new system of self-censorship in Russia and consolidate support from the conservative segment of Russian society around itself. This chapter will go beyond Pussy Riot’s performance and the trial alone to contextualize these events in the political atmosphere of the country at that time.

Dick Captured by the FSB. Art group Voina. 2010

On June 14, 2010, in St. Petersburg, the activists of the art group Voina painted a giant phallus on Liteiny Bridge at night, so that when the drawbridge was raised the figure rose in front of the city’s central Federal Security Service (FSB) office. The coordinated action was called Dick Captured by the FSB!. It measured 65 by 27 meters and was made in 23 seconds by 9 people. Later, two fire engines tried to wash the graffiti off using hoses. One of the participants was detained for two days (“2010-2011. Dick Captured,” 2010). On April 7, 2011, the action won an award in the field of contemporary art and Voina received 400,000 rubles (the equivalent of around 10,000 euros at the time)(“Exchange rates for” 2011). The art group then donated that money to the human rights organization Agora to help political prisoners (“Voina spent Innovation Award,” 2011). Lawyers of this organization would later also defend Nadezhda Tolokonnikova in court, a member of Voina and later also Pussy Riot. On December 31, 2011, a half an hour before the new year, Voina set fire to a police car—this was their seventh already, by that point in time (“Art group Voina,” 2012). Following this, a criminal case was opened against the art group on the basis of hooliganism, but nobody actually ended up being prosecuted (“Art-group Voina,” n.d.).

On February 21, 2012, a punk prayer named Mother of God, Drive Putin Away was performed by the feminist punk group Pussy Riot at the Cathedral of Christ the Savior in Moscow. For having done this, two members of the group received two years in prison under the ‘hooliganism’ law, a third participant was given two years’ probation ("The Case of," 2017). Their performance lasted 30 seconds, they did not damage any property, and the women also did not resist when they were taken out of the temple (Kichanova, 2012, ch.9 para.4; Idov, 2012). Between Voina burning the aforementioned police car and Pussy Riot’s performance in the church, only two months had passed, and their cases were examined on the basis of the same law.

The struggle between Russian artists and the Russian-Orthodox Church did not begin with Pussy Riot. For example, as part of a performance, reproductions of Russian-Orthodox icons were cut through with an axe in 1998, and the exhibitions Beware, Religion! in 2003 and Forbidden Art in 2006 were both fined and closed based on a law in Russia on the protection of its religious community’s feelings (Kichanova, 2012, ch.10 para.1). Notably, the person who had initiated the lawsuit would later also claim to have been a victim in the Pussy Riot case. (Kichanova, 2012, ch.10 para.2, ch.17 para.1).

Pussy Riot's Punk Prayer, Moscow, 2012

Yet, it was the performance at the Cathedral of Christ the Savior that was so highly resonant, and the women who participated in it received real terms in prison. During Pussy Riot's trial, two significant events took place: the law on the protection of the religious community’s feelings was made stricter, and a multitude of pressure groups arose that declared they would protect conservative and pro-government values (Naylor, 2017). Going forward, the maximum punishment under the revised law on the protection of the religious community became three years of imprisonment instead of the three months specified in its original iteration (Samokhina & Korchenkova, 2013).

Human rights experts concluded that the law does not describe what ‘insulting the feelings of believers’ means clearly enough and that, as a result, it can be interpreted far too loosely (Samokhina & Korchenkova, 2013). Commenting on the revised law, Nikolai Svanidze, a member of the Civic Chamber of Russia, stated that ‘the most flawed laws are those in which it is not exactly defined what a person can be punished for. When it comes to such a delicate matter as feelings, any citizen could be punished’ (Samokhina & Korchenkova, 2013).

To fully understand why Pussy Riot’s performance became such a significant event, it is critical to situate it within the political context in Russia at the time. The period between the end of 2011 and the beginning of 2012 was when Putin campaigned for presidency once again following a five-year interval during which was the prime-minister. His presidential nomination was accompanied by intense protests, and the opposition’s political activity around this time was at its highest relative to the decade before it (“Protests in Moscow,” 2012). The rallies held in Moscow in December 2011 were the largest since of its kind since the early 1990s (A Russian awakening, 2011). People took to the streets throughout Putin’s campaign to voice their dissatisfaction with the upcoming election. As this was taking place, the Russian-Orthodox Church became an active participant in politics (“The Russian-Orthodox Church,” 2012). It agitated people to vote for Putin and urged them not to join protests against the government (Haskins, 2015, p. 234; Expertmus, 2012).

Protest for fair elections March 6, 2012. Photo by Sergey Fadeichev / ITAR-TASS.

Shortly before the elections, the news organization Gazeta.ru published an article on how the politics and role of television channels changed in the weeks ahead of the vote (Mikhailov, 2011). One of the interviewed television channel managers explained that half of their censorship was self-censorship; they tried not to get into trouble and avoided causing controversy to sidestep potential conflicts (Mikhailov, 2011). According to the interviewees in the article, the managers of television channels and individual programs would typically intuitively decide whether to invite one or another politician for airtime, and only in contentious cases would they contact the officials of the presidential administration that supervised television (Mikhailov, 2011). Though mass protests continued even after the elections (up until the end of 2012), state-run television channels ignored these events almost entirely (Dzhanopoladova, Pavlova, & Tatarsky, 2017; Mikhailov, 2011). Thus, people could primarily only get information about what was taking place across the country through the Internet, where there was still active coverage of the ongoing events.

Given this context, it becomes apparent that Pussy Riot’s performance at the church was yet another extension of the political atmosphere in the country at the time. The group actively posted about their work on their website and on YouTube, which made it possible for them to gain popularity and followers without needing air-time on state-run media. The place where Pussy Riot’s famous performance was held is not likely to have been chosen at random. The Cathedral of Christ the Savior is the most important Russian-Orthodox cathedral in Russia. During services held by the Patriarch on important holidays, the cathedral is attended by the country’s most prominent officials, including Vladimir Putin himself (Haskins, 2015, p. 234).

In an interview, the members of Pussy Riot said that their performance was a political gesture in response to the Patriarch urging Russian-Orthodox citizens not to participate in the protests against Putin’s third term as president (Lun, 2012). They held a similar performance in another church just two days before the one that got them arrested, and they posted a video on YouTube about both performances that went viral (Lun, 2012). Ignoring their performance was impossible. It is said that Russian culture tends to respect demonstrations of power by the state, and if the government had not responded harshly enough, its supporters might have concluded that the state could not protect their values (Kichanova, 2012, ch.12 para.1). After the performance, one of Pussy Riot's members commented that they thought the Patriarch must have called Putin to ask how to punish them (Kichanova, 2012, ch.10 para.6).

Pussy Riot did not expect such a tough reaction from the religious community, because they did not aim to offend religious people’s feelings but wanted to open a discussion about the role of the Church in Russia (Lun, 2012; Kichanova, 2012, ch.16 para.4). Three investigations were carried out during the trial, of which two concluded that Pussy Riot’s performance was not an insult to the feelings of believers or a form of extremism (Kichanova, 2012, ch.14 para.17). It is worth noting that even though Pussy Riot’s members were convicted under the article for hooliganism, during their trial it was emphasized that their performance caused moral harm to religious people (Haskins, 2015, p. 239). Witnesses said that they did not hear ‘drive Putin away’ but blasphemy against God and the Church (Kichanova, 2012, ch.16 para.4, 19). The lawsuit avoided labelling the performance as a political issue and instead found issue in its alleged amoral and blasphemous content (Pussy Riot’s verdict, 2012). In state-run media outlets, Pussy Riot was not depicted as government oppositionists but as provocateurs who sought to undermine Russian culture, society, and ultimately Russia itself (Haskins, 2015, p. 239). Nadezhda Tolokonnikova and Maria Alekhina were arrested on March 5, the morning following the presidential elections (Lun, 2012).

Their performance became all the more resonant due to the government’s and Russian-Orthodox Church’s reaction. It became a matter of significance for the entire Russian art world, because it explicitly made artists political opponents in the eyes of the government. The real performance was not what Pussy Riot did at the church but the subsequent trial, which became the performance's logical conclusion and revealed the government’s attitude toward anyone who would challenge its legitimacy publicly. Because of its tough reaction, the Russian government also lost credibility internationally. Even though 86 percent of the world’s news agencies wrote about this matter at the time, it allowed for the consolidation of support from Russian conservatives (“About Pussy Riot,” 2012; Lun, 2012). Naturally, the state was not intent on spreading the idea that the arrest of the members of Pussy Riot was a matter of censorship, especially in the domestic media. In all news coverage, emphasis was placed on the claim that the performance was anti-religion, against God, and against Russian-Orthodoxy (to which 75 percent of Russia’s population belongs) (“Press Release No. 1461,” 2010). At that point in time, the share of people actively attending church in the country was 31 percent (“Orthodox Russians more,” 2013). The assertion that the state did not censor artists but simply protected believers was reiterated everywhere. In response to criticism from the international community, it was argued that these artists would have been arrested and convicted in any country in Europe (Kichanova, 2012, ch.15 para.13). The majority of the political opposition as well as artists and activists called for the release of arrested members of Pussy Riot, because the severity of their punishment was not proportionate to their crime (Demidova, 2012). Most state channels drew parallels between liberals and the punk group, contending that supporting Pussy Riot meant that you were also against faith and the Church (“Hammer of Witches,” 2012).

Protests March 6, 2012. Photo by Ilya Varlamov.

According to Kichanova (2012), the real battle unfolded at the courthouse, where — for one of the first times — Russian-Orthodox activist groups began demonstrating and felt entitled to dictate their will to others. Since none of their acts of aggression were punished, it was a clear message that the government tacitly approved of such behavior. One religious activist, Sergei Yakimov, struck Tatiana Romanova in her face after she said that God will forgive everyone. She was later diagnosed to have suffered a concussion, dislocation of the jaw, and displacement of her teeth (Kichanova, 2012, ch.15 para.8). Dmitry Tsorionov openly called for people to attack anyone wearing T-shirts that said Free Pussy Riot on them, forcing people to take these off (Kichanova, 2012, ch.15 para.10). Even after he started participating these attacks himself, the police did not find anything unlawful about his actions (“The police refused,” 2012). In this manner, the kind of religious activists that would play such an important role in the censorship of the future artistic life in the country began to emerge. Violence and intimidation were their modus operandi against artists and other opponents. Over the course of three years, from May 2012 to July 2015, Tsorionov took part in about 60 acts of so-called political struggle that involved the use of violence (Volchek, 2015). He was never convicted for any of his actions apart from his attack on Manezh, where several works of art belonging to the state were destroyed, and as a result spent ten days in prison (“The man responsible,” 2015). One of God’s Will’s most influential patrons is the Russian-Orthodox Patriarch’s own press secretary, an important public figure in the Church (Volchek, 2015).

Because of the Pussy Riot trial, the state began to realize that contemporary art, by its provocative nature, had the potential to popularize protest among young people (Haskins, 2015, p. 229). The Russian Institute for Strategic Studies analyzed the impact of contemporary art on politics. It found that that artists are able to express their ideas vividly, and, in so doing, have the capacity to attract a large number of people (Vlaskin, 2015). Performances such as Pussy Riot’s as well as exhibitions can catalyze new discourses in society, and the digital era has made the spread ideas far easier and faster (Haskins, 2015, p. 228). In Russia’s own history, there are various examples of artists and poets being active participants in the 1917 revolution, using their work as tools in their efforts (Medinsky, 2015).

The strongest support for the current government comes from conservative society (Medinsky, 2015). To discredit any form of political protest by contemporary artists, they were portrayed as opponents of classical art and the cultural values of the country (Vlaskin, 2015). Any social criticism was declared to be outside the sphere of art (Vlaskin, 2015). It was claimed that the Church had become a target for contemporary artists, because they wanted to destroy religion and imitate the West (Vlaskin, 2015). In the aftermath of these events, the Russian government began actively using artists who channeled patriotism and traditional values (Vlaskin, 2015; Medinsky, 2015).

Photo by Vitaliy Raskalov. Protests March 6, 2012.

Government officials were able to successfully swing the events surrounding the Pussy Riot case in their favor and consolidate patriotic and religious communities. In the fight over traditional values and Russian-Orthodoxy, more stringent control over artists as well as aggression towards them became legitimate from a societal point of view. State-run media outlets declared all forms of sociopolitical criticism through art to be Western machinations aimed at the destruction of Russian culture and art (Haskins, 2015, p. 228). Hence, government critics and artists who addressed these issues became enemies of the state, as often happened in totalitarian societies (Segal, 2016).

Chapter 4

2012-2017

This chapter explores the development of the relationship between the Russian government and artists from 2012 to 2017. Its aim is to show that the foundations for a self-censored art community are being laid. The government has not focused on creating extensive restrictive measures as is characteristic of traditional censorship policy. Instead, it has relied on more indirect methods of influencing society, as the age of the Internet has made traditional strategies ineffective.

Contemporary Russian society is in a state of conflict that is reminiscent of the time of the Cold War, and the art world has become a focal point in this battle. To consolidate support around the incumbent regime, authorities and state-run media have been in a constant search of enemies that interfere with the state’s development. As in the USSR, enemies of the state can take on any shape. These could be ecological organizations that rely on funding from abroad but also artists making a movie about in the war in Ukraine (Sarukhanov, 2017; Volynsky, 2017). Artists are explicitly tasked with serving the government’s interests (Medinsky, 2015). The atmosphere that artists in contemporary Russia have to work in can be characterized by a high degree of patriotism, religiosity, and a deficit of alternative political points of view as a result of a scarcity of independent news organizations.

Finding evidence of self-censorship is extremely difficult, because it is primarily the internal decision of an artist (Phillips, 2016, p. 218). If an artist self-censors their work, nobody but they can be sure of whether it happened or not. As specified in an earlier chapter of this thesis, self-censorship can be seen as the actions that individuals, organizations, or a community take to curb or somehow compromise expression in response to an internalized fear of potential punishment, abuse, or financial loss; and in order to earn the (tacit) approval of their peers, the public, or authorities. By identifying these components in Russian society, it is possible to indicate elements of self-censorship at work there.

Illustration for E. Zamyatin's novel "We" by Vukolov.1989

In addition, to get an idea of the atmosphere in which self-censorship is cultivated, it is possible to examine novels such as Nineteen Eighty-four by George Orwell or We by Yevgeny Zamyatin. Both were written in the 20th century with less than 30 years separating them. They describe totalitarian cultures and their influence on society. Both show a toxic environment in which the individual is denied freedom and autonomy. In ‘We’, even the names and surnames of people were replaced by letters and numbers. The USSR at the height of Stalin’s repression can also serve as an example of this kind of society. It was a state where people understood that criticism of the existing system was impossible, and any form of contestation of this would be punished. In that world, artists existed to serve the interests of the government. In order to survive, they had to control their ideas and words. In both of these books, the government is fighting perceived internal and external enemies, as in the USSR and in contemporary Russia.

The main characters in Orwell’s and Zamyatin’s seminal novels are keenly aware that any manifestation of dissent would be punished and that they need to carefully monitor their actions as well as their thoughts to avoid showing doubt in the governing powers. Constant propaganda and the fear of punishment make people hide their thoughts and ideas from each other too. In such a society, it is not only impossible to criticize the established order but freedom of speech is curbed as well. That is, unless you are ready to pay for it with your life, like Osip Mandelstam did for his poem about Stalin at the height of Stalin’s repressions. He did not publish it; that was impossible, of course. Mandelstam simply read it to his friends. Everybody present in the room at that moment understood what would follow, that there would be punishment, and that, one way or another, somebody would go to the police (Minejazeva, 2014).

While it is not possible to directly compare present-day Russia to the Soviet Union under Stalin or the dystopian societies described by Orwell and Zamyatin, some parallels can still be drawn. In contemporary Russia, artists are relentlessly scrutinized by the media as well as pressure groups, and their actions are overseen by the Ministry of Culture. As a result, they are forced to be cautious in everything they say and do if they value their ability to work, find funding, and wish to avoid being punished for their work.

As addressed in the previous chapter, artists came to be seen as political opponents by the Russian government after the 2012 presidential election and the Pussy Riot case. Following these events, artists began to be pressured by the state as well as groups who thought themselves to have the authority to decide what constituted art and what did not. The atmosphere that this created has been very aggressive towards art and is, in certain ways, reminiscent of how officials controlled artists in the USSR. It is aggressive, because it does not allow artists to be critical of the existing regime without fear of being ridiculed, attacked, or banned. However, it is not possible to punish or ban everyone and, at the same time, preserve the appearance of a democratic society in which there is freedom of expression (in life or art). Therefore, the state has sought to retain control over artists while maintaining a façade of freedom. It has done so by encouraging specific ideas and selectively punishing others. Encouragement typically takes the form of financing and awards handed out by state organizations. The ways in which artists are punished are quite diverse: from bullying in the state-run media to direct attacks on artists and their exhibitions or performances, financial audits, criminal prosecutions, or any combination of the aforementioned.

By channeling art as a medium for the circulation of patriotic sentiments, the Russian government appears to recognize its influence. At the same time, however, it denies political or socially-critical art the very status of art. While this may seem at odds, according to Freedberg (2016) “to censor . . . a work is to testify to its hold over its public. It is to acknowledge the work’s seductiveness . . . and to admit that what should not have power actually does,” so the government’s efforts to stem certain forms of art or declare that these are not valid as art only further supports the claim that authorities are keenly aware of art’s capacity to inspire, rally, or influence society (p. 68).

The rest of this chapter examines several contemporary cases in which artists were persecuted for their statements or work. These cases are emblematic of the wider toxic atmosphere in which Russian artists, filmmakers, photographers, writers, and other artists are working today.

State orders, censorship, and bullying in the media

According to gossips that were actively discussed in the press, the scandal that is described further did not begin with the official speech of Konstantin Raikin, a director, in defense of the freedom of art. It began earlier yet, with the Ministry of Culture remarking that Raikin should not permit plays about a gay teenage boy in a state theater (Kholmogory, 2016).

In October 2016, one of the most famous Russian directors and head of the Satyricon theater, Konstantin Raikin, delivered a twelve-minute speech about censorship in Russian art (“Konstantin Raikin’s speech,” 2016). This monologue opened a discourse in Russian society that drew in many public figures, including the renowned director Andrei Zvyagintsev, the press secretary of the president Dmitry Peskov, and the minister of culture Vladimir Medinsky. Raikin expressed indignation at the frequent attacks on artists, exhibitions, and performances by religious and pro-government groups that, in so doing, were actively censoring art, while authorities neglected to do anything about it (“Konstantin Raikin’s speech,” 2016).

The Kremlin’s official reaction to Raikin’s words was relatively reserved. Asked to comment on what Raikin said, the president’s press secretary Dmitry Peskov replied that ‘censorship, as such, is unacceptable ... Naturally, if the government gives money to some artists, then we have the right to designate a particular topic. This is not censorship; it shouldn’t be confused with a government order' (“Konstantin Raikin’s words,” 2016). Yet, he did not comment on why the government had not punished activists who disrupted exhibitions and performances. In so doing, Peskov only further underlined that the government wishes to avoid drawing attention to the crimes and violence directed against the art world (Zvyagintsev, 2016). Revealingly, the chairman of the Public Council to the Ministry of Culture, Pavel Poszhigailo, criticized Raikin for having raised the issue of censorship to a political level, when, in his view, the issue could have been discussed in the council (“The situation with Raikin,” 2016).

Public opinion quickly split into two camps: those who supported Raikin and those who sought to distance themselves from him as quickly as possible. Andrei Zvyagintsev, who spoke out in support of Raikin, published an open letter in which he asserted that the government should not consider the government budget as its own money: ‘They forgot, surprisingly easily erased from their mind, the simple and obvious idea that this is not their money but ours. Everyone’s. The money, with which they order their political advertisements, is taken from the people' (Zvyagintsev, 2016). He added that ‘bans of plays, bans of exhibitions, bans on publications: all of this is censorship. It’s just incredible how easily concepts are being replaced. Nobody even raises an eyebrow. We say: this is censorship, they say: this is a government order. And we are even advised not to confuse these concepts' (Zvyagintsev, 2016). The Kremlin responded to this as well and stated that they manage the money of the state in the interests of the state itself (“Konstantin Raikin’s words,” 2016)’. The scandal took on such proportions that even Putin had to address it at a meeting of the Presidential Council for Culture and Art (Juditskaya, 2016).

In response to his speech, Raikin was publicly bullied by state media. The director was heavily criticized by most of the country’s famous media personalities, including head of Chechnya Ramzan Kadyrov (“Kadyrov supported the Khirurg,” 2016). The director’s speech was even associated with zoophilia; the absence of censorship was claimed to lead to uncontrolled depravity and allow you to have sex with animals on a theater stage (“Loza about Panin and Raikin,” 2016). This rather bizarre comparison was made by multiple famous persons in several of the largest media networks in Russia. Moreover, in most newspaper headlines Raikin was referred to in diminutive form (Kostya rather than Konstantin), which in Russia is an unambiguously humiliating way of being referred to in the state-run media. These headlines and the way in which Raikin and his speech about censorship were discussed shaped a certain attitude towards him in society: his words ceased to be taken seriously.

Attacks and public criticism

In the USSR, there was a form of criticism whereby letters from the public were published in newspapers. These were primarily furious and vicious remarks directed against certain people, artists, or writers. After Pasternak was awarded a Nobel Prize in literature for Doctor Zhivago, he was subjected to an immense wave of criticism in such letters. A large compilation of these was published in the newspaper Literaturnaya Gazeta under the heading Anger and resentment. Soviet people condemn the actions of Boris Pasternak. The titles of the published letters speak for themselves: Commissioned Slander, From Aestheticism to Moral Collapse, A Shameful Act (Polivanov, n.d.). As a result of the pressure he faced, Pasternak ended up refusing his Nobel Prize (Shapiro, 2013).

Photo by Alexandros Avramidis

Nowadays, while such letters are not as popular or common, the press is still extremely influential in shaping public opinion, as seen with what happened to Konstantin Raikin. Public indignation, once expressed in letters to newspapers, has assumed new shapes. After the Pussy Riot’s trial, some patriotic and religious groups took it upon themselves to express what they claimed was public opinion by way of attacks and threats on artists. Because these groups are not punished, they feel legitimized in their activities and are even further emboldened by statements made by officials on this subject. For example, Vladimir Putin, in a discussion about censorship in Russia, said that everything that is not prohibited by law should be filtered out by society itself, with minimal government influence (“Putin about censorship”, 2017). This can be viewed as an implicit statement of consent for the attacks committed against artists by activist groups.

This wave of developments since 2012 made it possible for artistic life to be censored by activist groups. These are mainly religious or patriotic groups like as God’s Will and the Officers of Russia, respectively. Artists cannot raise subjects in their work that appear to critically assess the Church, the history of the country, the actions of the Russian government in Ukraine, and many other topics. This raises the following question: if artists refuse to address these topics, is it because they are truly disinterested, or is it because they are afraid of provoking attacks on themselves?

Daria Fedorova made a project named The Journey of the Human Microbiome. In Russian churches, it is customary to kiss icons after performing a prayer. The artist wanted to examine what effect this had on a microscopic level, so she took swabs of icons from Moscow churches to be analyzed. Her project is composed of images of the bacteria that remain after people kiss these icons. According to Daria, it was not the intent of her project to paint the Church in a negative light. Nevertheless, she expects that she cannot have it exhibited anywhere, because the Church would see it as an open provocation (Petkova, 2017). Thus artists seem to create projects with an awareness of what can be published and what not, which topics can be addressed and exhibited in galleries and which not. This all, of course, takes place in the absence of explicit prohibitions.

In the following, I would like to contrast two examples. In the one, an exhibition continued to operate after it had been attacked by conservative activists, while in the other the exhibition was forced to close under pressure. Their differences in financial dependence on the government appear to have been an important differentiator for the outcome of each attack.

Photography exhibition, bathed in paint. Photo by Alexander Vasyukovich

On September 28 and 29, 2016, there were attacks on a photo exhibition at the Sakharov Center, a human rights organization, in Moscow. Work from the competition Direct Look was shown there, and among the competition’s winners were the photographers Alexander Vasyukovich and Sergei Loiko. They both worked amid the conflict in southeastern Ukraine and photographed Ukrainian soldiers (War in Ukraine, 2016). On the exhibition’s opening day someone sprayed the photos from Ukraine with red paint. The ruined images were then replaced by sheets of white paper that said that works devoted to the war in Ukraine had been shown there. The next day, several more attackers appeared demanding the exhibition’s closure, and they removed the sheets that hung in the place of the pictures. At the same time, an inspection of the Sakharov Center was initiated by the public prosecutor’s office for propagandizing Ukrainian nationalism. Even though the name of attacker from the opening day was almost immediately published in the press, police did not investigate him. Despite these events, the Sakharov Center refused to close the exhibition (Berg, 2016).

The second example is the exhibition Jock Sturges: Absence of Shame at the Lumiere Brothers Gallery in Moscow. Because of a suspicion that photographs of naked children were on display at the exhibition, members of the patriotic activist group Officers of Russia blocked the entrance to the exhibition and splashed the photos on display with urine (Igritsky, 2016; Antonova, 2017). If someone suspected that the exhibition violated the laws of the Russian Federation, they would be within their right to officially request further examination of its contents. However, the exhibited work did not depict nude children and was not found to be in violation of any laws (Antonova, 2017). Nevertheless, the ordeal ended with the exhibition’s closure, because the gallery was unable to contest these attacks.

In both cases, the organizers of the exhibitions did not receive the protection they needed (and should have been given) from law enforcement or the government. After all, it was not the galleries that violated the law but those who attacked them. Yet, in neither the first nor in the second case were the aggressors even investigated.

The Sakharov Center is a public organization that owes its continued operation to international grants and can therefore function without the support of the state. In short, it cannot be cut off from funding as a result of such attacks. The Lumiere Brothers Gallery, in contrast, is a private gallery that must survive on its own, which means it is largely dependent on its relationship with the authorities as well as pressure groups. Bad press may negatively impact the number of visitors, and scandals can affect potential art buyers.

After these events, the number of visitors at the Lumiere Brothers Gallery fell dramatically. The greater an organization’s dependence on the state’s favor and the state-run media, the more readily one submits to the conditions of highly-organized pressure groups or influential institutions such as the Church. If such controversies concern government-owned establishments (which encapsulates the vast majority of theaters and museums in Russia), any case of an insulted public will have its consequences ("All state theaters," 2017).

Opera Tannhäuser. Novosibirsk State Opera and Ballet Theatre. Photo by Viktor Dmitriev.

Another pronounced example of how extensively religious groups and the government exert influence over the art community can be found in the case of the opera Tannhäuser at the Novosibirsk Opera and Ballet Theater. The head of this theater Boris Mezdrich as well as director Timofey Kulyabin were dismissed after the local religious community declared that their feelings were offended by the theater’s staging of Wagner’s famous opera (“Novosibirsk theater director,” 2015). Kulyabin and Mezdrich were accused of the deliberately desecrating religious symbols in public (“The court acquitted,” 2015). The initiator of this scandal was a group of Russian-Orthodox Christians who were supported by the metropolitan bishop of Novosibirsk—even though none of them had actually seen the opera in question (“Novosibirsk theater director,” 2015). In the end, the court did not find evidence of any offense to the feelings of the religious community (“The court acquitted,” 2015). Nonetheless, the Ministry of Culture made an official statement wherein it called upon the opera’s organizers to apologize to the religious people whose feelings were hurt (“Ministry of Culture,” 2015). Moreover, the Ministry ordered the play to be shut down and for Mezdrich and Kulyabin to be fired (“Novosibirsk theater director,” 2015). It then also issued a statement that spoke about the consequences for those who would not obey: ‘While we consider censorship to be impermissible, we remind you of our right—our responsibility even—in the scope of fulfilling the Foundations of State Cultural Policy to undertake administrative and financial measures against state cultural institutions, including cutting them of funding, when faced with extraordinary circumstances' (“Ministry of Culture,” 2015).

In cases of attacks against artists, art, or galleries the government has either taken a neutral position or acted on the side of the Church and patriotic groups. The authorities’ steadfast inaction signals to the public that everyone has the moral and legal right to act out against art in whatever way they deem appropriate. Furthermore, by firing or denying funding to those who do not agree with the rules of the game, the government is able to claim that official censorship does not exist and at the same time control the topics that can be freely spoken about. The Ministry of Culture even has a blacklist of undesirable artists. Photos of this list, signed by the deputy minister of culture Vladimir Aristarkhov, recently got out on the Internet (“Black lists,” 2017). The way people are described in these is akin to a dossier from the KGB’s archive: ‘Is inclined to erode traditional Russian spiritual as well as moral values and attempts to falsify Russian history through their art,' 'Evidently shares an oppositional political outlook’ (“Black lists,” 2017).

An important discovery that came out of the discourse raised by Raikin is that that officials still see public expenditures on art and culture as government orders. Simply put, artists must serve the interests of the government if they receive state money, similar to how it was in the USSR in Stalin’s time, when government orders were the only way in which artists and the government interacted. Furthermore, since most theaters belong to the state and their budget is subsidized for up to 75% by the government, their repertoire is expected to meet the interests of the government ("All state theaters," 2017). Hence, they need to put on performances that are in the interests of those who provide them with funding.

Patriotism. National identity. Traditional values.

Art can be dangerous to those with political power. Mintcheva (2016) asserts that “as a political actor, [art] can challenge social attitudes but also be complicit with the structures of power. It can open up new ways of thinking but also offend deeply held beliefs” (p. 207). Having recognized the power of art and its capacity to influence a society, the Russian government has sought to revise the rules of the game in the art world. The objectives that the government set out for artists are outlined quite clearly in an article by the Russian minister of culture wherein he frames the purpose of art as a representation of national identity (Medinsky, 2015). He discusses artists’ responsibilities toward society and the values that ought to be shared by all members of the art community. Aside from this, it is emphasized that only artwork of a specific content can supported by the Ministry of Culture and the government. According to Medinsky this is not censorship; instead, it is claimed to be the government’s duty toward society (Medinsky, 2015).. While it may not be censorship per se, funding specific forms of art that promote ideas echoed by the government while excluding art that offers a less than optimistic view of the country’s leadership is what Hassid (2008) refers to as “marketization,” which fosters self-censorship by opening pathways for personal or organizational success in exchange for acting in the government's interests (p. 418).

Vladimir Putin and Patriarch Kirill. Photo by Alexei Druzhinin.

Mirroring similar structures that were in place in the USSR, it is from Medinsky's article that a system of government orders exists in contemporary Russia, whereby the government delineates exactly what kind of art they are willing to subsidize. However, there is no relationship between the criteria by which financial support is provided and the (expected) quality of the work. Rather, content is the deciding factor (Medinsky, 2015). Only projects that adhere to ideas like serving the fatherland and patriarchal values are encouraged and financed (Medinsky, 2015). In his article, the minister of culture shifts the accountability for this restrictive policy to society, stating that when making such decisions he bases these on the president’s high approval rate (Medinsky, 2015). However, the people who support Putin tend to endorse conservative values, which is naturally also reflected in their expectations of art (Medinsky, 2015). This generates a closed loop: people support the president, because they are conservative, so they are presented with conservative sentiments and art, these people thus continue to exist in a bubble of conservatism and support the president for maintaining the status quo. Consequently, there is no room for shock or innovation; nothing is allowed to upset or offend the public, and yet another engine for public discourse of societal issues is shut down. Like in the Soviet Union, artistic experimentation is severely impeded (Segal, 2016, p. 65; Medinsky, 2015).

Currently, Russian national identity is molded around two central pillars: The Second World War and religion. More than any other subjects, these two cannot be made fun of, reevaluated, or criticized in the slightest. They represent timeless ideals. Hence, the supposed desecration of the most important Russian-Orthodox cathedral in the country became an important element of the trial against Pussy Riot, and exhibitions like We Won are closed for reconsidering how the Second World War and the Soviet victory are perceived (“Police destroy the exhibition,” 2015).

In the search for its identity through art, the government rigorously oversees which ideas are publicly shown and how the past, present, and future of the country are represented. The belief underlying this is that no mistakes could have been made in this beautiful country’s past, nor are they made now; its history is glorious, as is its future. Patriarchal traditions and paternalism are inserted into society through art. This can be found in cinema and monuments (Segal, 2016, p. 111). Whereas going to theaters or museums may not be regular pastimes for a share of the population, movies and grandiose sculptures are in the public’s eye on a more regular basis (p. 111).

Photo Igor Stomakhin

In the past few years, a large number of monuments celebrating Russian heads of state as well as religious leaders have been erected across the country. In 2017, the Rulers’ Avenue was constructed under the patronage of the Ministry of Culture (Zotova, 2017). Located in the very center of Moscow, it features sculptures of Russian princes, tsars, and Soviet leaders—monuments to Stalin even have appeared in several cities. In addition to this, monuments to various representatives of the Russian-Orthodox Church — from the first Patriarch, Job, up to the current Patriarch Kirill — are on display at Cathedral of Christ the Savior until the end of 2019 (Berishvili, 2017). A new monument to Vladimir the Great, the baptizer of Rus’, was also constructed near the Kremlin in 2016. In his speech at the monument’s unveiling, Putin called Vladimir the Great ‘the spiritual founder of the Russian state’, and described him as ‘the gatherer and protector of Russian lands’ (Zamakhina, 2016). The latter title was also brazenly ascribed to Putin himself in various media outlets following the Crimean conflict (Grynszpan, 2017).

These kinds of historical monuments often serve political goals and constitute the canon of collective memory (Segal, 2016, p. 111). Using monuments, the government “define the message,” by presenting historic figures and events as things to look up to or follow; they project an idealized version of history onto the present (p. 111). The Russian government has been idealizing (former) leaders and treating them as if they were sacred or personifications of timeless ideals (p. 111). Authority itself has become sanctified (Ponomarev, & Berg, 2018). This notion is supported by the Russian-Orthodox Church. In the words of Patriarch Kirill, all power comes from God (Zubov, 2012). This way of thinking is deeply rooted in Russian history and is perhaps best encapsulated in the old expression tsar-batyushka, which roughly translates to 'the tsar, our father'. The ruler takes care of his subjects as a father would care for his children (Timchenko, Kolpakov, & Kremer, 2017). However, in this concept lies another implicit but nonetheless universally-understood agreement that, in return, the children are prohibited from criticizing their father and must obey him fully and unquestioningly (Timchenko, Kolpakov, & Kremer, 2017). These monuments justify the construction of a new Russian national identity that reconciles the USSR and religion (Segal, 2016, p. 127; Timchenko, Kolpakov, & Kremer, 2017). Ostensibly, this is what the minister of culture meant when he spoke of values that ought to be shared everyone in the art world (Medinsky, 2015).

Within this context, it becomes more evident why the movie The Death of Stalin by Armando Lannucci was prohibited from being shown in Russia. It satirizes and makes fun of the events surrounding Stalin’s death as well as the ensuing fight for power in the vacuum that was left. The idea that an authority figure, the head of state, can be funny or even absurd is an unacceptable notion among officials. The decision to withdraw The Death of Stalin’s license to be shown was made just two days ahead of its Russian premiere. Members of parliament to whom the film was screened in private claimed to have seen signs of extremism in it, but an official reason as to why it was forbidden was not given (Berg, 2018; Sulim, 2018).

Following the scandal surrounding this movie, members of the State Duma (the lower house of parliament) suggested the establishment of a so-called morality committee with the ability to decide which movies should or should not be permitted distribution. The reason they seek to form such a committee is because they did not have a reason or formal justification to prohibit The Death of Stalin or even the authority to do so (Ponomarev & Berg, 2018) (Drapeko, 2018). Like in the Pussy Riot trial, authorities are actively searching for a any justification to prohibit The Death of Stalin, because there is none yet. Despite everything, Stalin is attributed the same sanctity as other leaders, and, for this reason, any attempts to discuss his role in history are suppressed in modern Russia (Ponomarev & Berg, 2018).

Controversy also surrounded the release of the film Matilda by director Alexey Uchitel, which depicted the extramarital relationship of Tsar Nikolai II with Matilda Kshesinskaya. In protest of its release, Russian-Orthodox activists threatened the director, set a theater in Yekaterinburg on fire, attacked the director’s studio, and lit cars on fire in front of the building where the director’s lawyer worked. The outrage caused various theaters to refuse showing the movie, even though it was not officially forbidden (Luhn, 2017).

The Death of Stalin and Matilda set a new precedent. It has become completely unclear what could cause offense and to whom. This could be religious people and veterans, as well as Russian people in general. It is unclear what can and cannot be made fun of, what subjects can and cannot be talked about. Even the director of the movie Burnt by the Sun (and its sequel), who addressed Stalin’s repressions in his 1994 film and even mocked Stalin by dunking him into a pie in his 2010 film, condemned the satire of Stalin (Ponomarev & Berg, 2018; Mikhalkov and Bortko 2018). All of this is quite confusing. The Death of Stalin’s prohibition was based on value judgments rather than rules or regulations. Evidently, even if there is no applicable law, a committee could theoretically be set up to make such decisions. The minister of culture does not believe in artists’ loyalty toward their country without controlling them. In the aforementioned article of his, he recalls how the creative elite supported the 1917 revolution as well as the 1991 attempt to a Soviet coup d’état, when artists ended up at the center of the forces that destroyed the state (Medinsky, 2015).

Beyond being controlled in what kind of work they produce, artists find themselves under the constant pressure of propaganda. As has been made apparent by now, Russian officials have been going down a similar path as in the USSR. Art that expresses criticism of the status quo and dares to address societal or political flaws is claimed to be trampling traditional values. Such works of art are purportedly organized by Russia’s enemies with the aim of undermining Russian culture by implanting Western values through contemporary art. This train of thought has given birth to the atmosphere of fear and confrontation that now permeates the public discourse on art, whereby you are either on the right side or labeled an enemy of the state. This, once more, is a repetition of phenomena that date back to the USSR — the idea that the world is seeking to destroy a glorious society, so its people must stay united and eliminate internal enemies as ruthlessly as external ones. Concurrently, a campaign for the resurrection of patriotic art that praises the achievements and victories of the past has steadily been gaining traction.

Segal (2016) discusses how during times of political crisis artists are urged to provide spiritual support in this struggle. Art was (and continues to be) seen as an expression of the national identity and its values in a form that is accessible to the wide public (Segal, 2016, pp. 20-21). Contemporary art is open to interpretation and often cannot be interpreted in a singular obvious way, which makes it less useful as a tool to influence the mainstream public and is why it is often claimed to be a destructive force. These kinds of attitudes and policies towards artists can be recognized all through the 20th century. In times of war, art has consistently been employed as a tool for propaganda (p. 13).

Politicians have long believed that wars are a way of reinvigorating national values and culture, of mending domestic schisms and uniting society (p. 21). Perhaps this is the reason why — for a long time already — the media in Russia has been reiterating the idea that the country is in a state of ideological war. Linguists note that a high degree of aggression can be found in the statements of Russian officials. Their speeches often contain expressions that mirror Soviet propaganda from the 1930s to 1940s (Granina, 2017). On a daily basis, the country is being told that its enemies are all around and that people should stay vigilant and united (Granina, 2017).

A few years ago, the Russian minister of culture declared that he has a negative disposition towards art that is not understandable to the majority of the Russian population (“Russia will have”, 2013). Perhaps unsurprisingly then, in 2014 the Ministry of Culture shuttered the department that was in charge of supporting contemporary art (Karmunin, 2014,).

It turns out that, just as in the USSR, it is impossible to work entirely freely; the main difference is that there is no government-sanctioned list of prohibited subjects. Artists are instead invited to draft this list themselves by watching the news. In reality, free artists pose very little political risk to the incumbent regime since Putin’s approval rating, as of 2017, stands at 83% (“Activities of government institutions,” 2017). The only sense in fighting artists is to remind people that there are external as well as internal enemies with whom the government is at war and that it needs the public’s support in these efforts—just like the enemies of the people in the USSR or in the dystopian societies described by George Orwell and Yevgeny Zamyatin. Now, however, the enemies are liberal artists out to supposedly destroy the country from within, impose dubious values, and destroy the moral values of Russian culture. The artist has become an internal enemy against whom aggression can and must be directed. Therefore, to survive in this climate, Russian contemporary artists have little choice but to play by the rules of the new paradigm or risk being ridiculed in the press or attacked by the public while the government and law enforcement turn a blind eye.

Chapter 5

My self-censorship

Director Timofey Kulyabin said in an interview that after the scandal surrounding Tannhäuser and its investigation, he would try to overcome the fear that developed in him during his trial. He described it as follows: ‘fear is a property of human nature, and it is also very easy to play on that fear. Thus, if you wish to remain a realist, self-censorship is to be expected, because, I repeat, man is already driven by fear—[fear] for his own destiny, prosperity. A seemingly primitive emotion but a destructive one...' (Alpatova, n.d.)

This chapter addresses my personal struggles and the reasons I self-censor. As stated earlier, discerning self-censorship in the art sphere is complicated by the fact that the decision to do so is made on a personal level; each individual will have their own incentives and disincentives to self-censor or not. In the previous sections the focus was primarily on exploring different layers of the mechanisms of self-censorship in Russia. In addition, they examined the roots of this phenomenon from a historical perspective and in light of more recent events. By engaging with the literature on censorship, exploring developments in the Russian art sphere, and providing a thick description of the synthesis of these, it was possible to approximate how self-censorship works in the country.

A different approach to identifying and describing self-censorship is to draw from artists’ own statements on the matter, to seek out what their motivations and fears were or still are. Such accounts are unfortunately difficult to obtain. Due to the complex nature of the driving forces behind self-censorship, people will often either prefer not to discuss it or may even consider it to be a positive force that is necessary (Groys, 2016, p. 211).

The reasons to self-censor are wholly dependent on the individual, but it is not possible to peek into other peoples’ internal decision-making process and to fully know their fears or anxieties without them openly talking about this. For this reason, I have taken my personal motivations to self-censor as the foundation to this section. I cannot expect or demand the same degree of candidness on this issue from others as I can from myself. It is their right to talk about what causes them to self-censor to the extent that they are willing to go and be as open as they wish. This study would not be complete without such a personal element. The question at the heart of this chapter and the one I posed other artists as well is: what drives me to censor myself, my work, or the places they are published?

It so happened that I was able to observe the current situation in Russia from two perspectives. I am both inside the Russian political system and outside of it. As an artist, I often feel like a person in two worlds: I am a Russian photographer but one who currently resides outside the country, so Russian viewers see me as a person who has left the country and now belongs to the West, whereas Western viewers simply consider me Russian. In effect, I do not belong to either of these worlds, because neither fully counts me as one of their own. Being outside of Russia, I can examine the state of the country, but because I have family there I still self-censor. This is one of the central reasons why I can do a thesis about self-censorship, and at the same time it is why I ask questions about the extent to which I censor myself in my work. I am not under the direct influence of the political system and propaganda of Russia anymore, so I am able to look at these critically. Nevertheless, it is my country, and I feel closely connected to what is taking place there. I read news about Russia in Western sources as well as Russian ones, I am on Russian social media, and my family as well as many of my friends are there. Because of this, I cannot be indifferent to what is taking place in the Russian art world and beyond. It does more than just worry me; it scares me, because I understand that the country is changing and possibly I will not have anywhere to return to, as I may not be able to find a place for myself in that new society. Additionally, nowadays moving abroad does not mean you are cutting your ties to your home country. The Internet gives us the ability to constantly be in touch with people far away, but, at the same time, it also makes escaping the sphere of influence of propaganda and news more difficult.

This close connection to Russia, my family, and my cultural background are all part of the foundation of my self-censorship. They have become the reasons why I watch what I say about Russia and where I say it. Over the course of writing this thesis I talked to several artists living in Russia about its subject matter. Each one of them would only speak on condition of anonymity.

I have heard quite regularly that it is not necessarily the self-censorship in the Russian art world that is interesting but artists’ fight against the political system. Resisting censorship and propaganda while being surrounded by it on a daily basis is immensely tough, however. Very few are able to cope with such pressure, and their numbers have gradually been whittled down. After Pussy Riot's now famous performance and a two-year prison sentence faced by two members of the group, they ceased their performances. They published a recording in which they announced that Pussy Riot was dead would stop their activities (“Public message from the group,” 2015). Nadezhda Tolokonnikova now lives and works in the United States and Europe (Taratuta, 2017). Maria Alyokhina is touring Europe and the Unites States with a play about Pussy Riot (Taratuta, 2017). Yekaterina Samutsevich, who received a suspended sentence, has disappeared from the public eye entirely. In one of the few interviews with her, she declared that her time with the group has ended (Taratuta, 2017). The artist Petr Pavlensky fled Russia and requested political asylum in France in 2017 (Petr Pavlensky emigrated,” 2017). Photographer Danila Tkachenko, a World Press Photo laureate and Kandinsky Prize nominee, has said in an interview that he was thinking about temporary emigrating after his Motherland project (Komissarova, 2017).

Many artists feel as if disagreeing with the government’s policies would mean having to leave the country, as the state expects complete subordination (“What do Russian artists,” 2016). This is echoed in the comments on self-censorship given to me by a Russian video artist. He said that nowadays only those who are prepared to emigrate are willing to act against the political system. It is especially dangerous to speak critically of Putin. When creating something particularly radical, you need to be ready for the Russian art world to reject you as it tries to rescue itself. So, if a project’s goal is to impress Western audiences, making a name for yourself there, and leaving the country, it is worth doing. If not, you need to accept the possibility that it might be the last project you make; nobody will want to be associated with you anymore.

When trying to define the concept of self-censorship, fear is often identified as a central driving force — fear of the repercussions of your actions, writing, photographs, or paintings. Fear is a relatively broad concept in itself and can encompass a near infinite number of consequences to be afraid of—from something as direct as punitive measures to financial losses, losing esteem, or social isolation. While acknowledging that fear is a common factor, I would argue that there is more nuance and complexity to be found in what provokes self-censorship beyond fear. In my own case, it is a chaotic amalgamation of forces that relate to my family and friends, my cultural background, career uncertainty, and a great many others. Moreover, these factors are often not independent of each other; they intersect and have multiplicative effects, producing a murky torrent of confusion. It is not possible to disentangle them entirely and point to a single overriding factor, because they overlap and are interwoven with aspects of my identity. Together, these become obstacles that may compel me to refrain from engaging in certain projects, abandoning them, avoid discussing them in certain contexts, or limit their publication to specific circles.

The factors that drive me to self-censor are manifold and differ depending on the subject matter. Nevertheless, there are several that tend to be more prominent influences, which I would like to draw attention to as these provide some insight into how self-censorship need not purely be driven by fear. Firstly, self-censorship can be driven by emotions. As most do, I care deeply about my mother. She is a highly patriotic person, an ardent proponent of Russia, and tends to watch political talk shows a tad too frequently. I know that my work that criticizes Russia would offend her, and I do not want to argue with my mother because of politics. For this reason, I have kept a large portion of my artistic life hidden from her.

The desire to avoid arguing with her is so simple that it is difficult to even call it self-censorship. Yet, on account of this I will not promote certain projects on social media, because I do not want her to find out about them. Self-censorship does not necessarily need to express itself as refraining from doing a certain project or changing it; limiting the sphere in which you are willing to speak about or promote your work is also way of censoring yourself. I may not personally be influenced by propaganda, but I am affected by it nonetheless due to the effect it has on my family. As a result, I am compelled to watch what I say and where I say it if I want to maintain a peaceful relationship with some of my relatives. Of course, my mother would not disavow me if I would suddenly start discussing politically-heated subjects with her more openly. She is already aware of my political views, but I would know that, in a way, I was hurting her feelings. These kinds of fears play into the government’s hand. What is even more frightening, however, is imagining that by criticizing Russia I could bring harm to my family. When it is possible to go to jail for sharing an image depicting a portrait of Putin being set on fire, it is easy to imagine a wealth of ways you could get youself or your family in trouble (“Found, reposted, in prison,” 2016).

Secondly, and in similar vein to the aforementioned, self-censorship can be motivated by friendship. Am I willing to lose some of my friends because of political views and beliefs? Are these beliefs worth that, or is it simpler to just separate art from friendship and avoid discussing sensitive political issues in certain contexts? It is not politeness but self-censorship. This is another form of self-imposed censorship that I am confronted with on a regular basis.

Thirdly, self-censorship can be prompted by cultural codes. A powerful layer of self-censorship that was etched into my brain—and has remained there to this day—by my Soviet childhood is that criticizing your country means betraying it. When you have grown up under a paternalistic value system in which the state is perceived to take care of you like a father would his children, it becomes quite difficult to address the government’s shortcomings. It is challenging to prove to Russian people that, despite your criticism of it, you still love the country and simply want to make it better. It is far easier to just avoid talking about such subjects altogether, because critical discourse is often resolved rather dichotomously; if you are not on their side you are instinctively seen as an enemy, an instrument of Western interference.

Finally, self-censorship can be a matter of seeking out success. If I criticize the country’s domestic or foreign politics, question how its history is perceived, or bring up sensitive social issues, I will not likely become a successful artist in Russia. Most likely I would be ostracized by most of the art community. Would I be willing to work like Bulgakov did, knowing that I will never become successful or recognized for my efforts and that there is only a slim chance for my work to reach its intended audience in the first place? Unless you have the option of travelling and finding work abroad, it is likely that the practical realities of being a professional artist in Russia will catch up to and overtake your personal ambitions for the country’s future. Even so, the vast majority of the Russian audience will not accept you if live in the West but criticize Russia; you will be considered an enemy for being paid by the West to act against your fatherland. Working with foreign funds is the most common accusation lodged against artists, political opposition, or human rights defenders, and it is one of the most serious (Sarukhanov, 2017).

Thus, regardless of whether you are based in the country itself or abroad, criticizing the country’s political system will not make you successful in Russia, which raises a several fundamental questions. If your work is liable to be met by strong pushback from the majority of the population, is it truly worth the price of enduring harsh criticism, insults, threats, or possible criminal persecution? Given Putin’s high approval rate and the difficulty of resisting an endless stream of propaganda, there is no tangible gain to be had from criticizing the government’s policies if you live in Russia (Possible Results of presidential,” 2017). How, then, do you avoid the temptation of incorporating more popular subjects in your work that will appeal to the public and officials alike? Though each person will reconcile these concerns differently, Russian artists are strongly pressured, coerced even, to concede any dissatisfaction they might harbor toward government policies or the state of the country.

In short, my self-censorship is the product of an inner voice that tells me not to address potentially objectionable topics. If this sounds very broad, that is because it is, indeed, broad. The ever-present possibility of drawing the ire of the government, religious community, conservatives, or any other majority group in the population compels a Russian artist to question their decisions at each and every turn, consider how their work might be perceived or (mis)interpreted, and weigh the risks of publishing provocative content against the reality of exposing oneself to anger, public shaming, social isolation, unemployment, incarceration, assault, or any combination of these. Self-censorship is a voice that whispers to me that my entire family is in Russia; that, even though I am abroad now, I will still have to return there and, by virtue of being a citizen, still need to answer to the government’s whims; that there will be very few who I will be able to show my portfolio to; and that, regardless of my convictions or ambitions, I still need able to afford a roof over my head. As evidenced by the complexity of it all, self-censorship is not black and white. It is multi-layered, a personalized paranoia, and it is difficult to describe yet also easy to let it become second nature and censor yourself unwittingly in order to simply cope. To understand that you’re self-censoring, you need to be honest to yourself first and foremost.

Even though I am situated outside the political system of Russia and its direct sphere of influence, I understand that I still censor myself, that I am still afraid of addressing certain topics. The people who live and work in a censored society describe its atmosphere as toxic. They compare it to the rules of a game, that, if they are not followed to the word, prevent you from even beginning to play; you simply need to come to terms with these conditions. You do not really have a choice, because otherwise society will turn its back on you. In private, many people will certainly express dissatisfaction toward the government, but such conversations are confined to the realm of dinner tables or private chats. We get used to thinking that it is better to keep quiet in public, because somebody might be offended or angered. In response to my questions about their experience of self-censorship, one interviewee remarked that she only expresses her political views in private conversations with close friends. In fact, she deliberately never reposts or even comments on negative press about the Russian-Orthodox Church or the government on social media. The reason for this being that she does not want to upset her friends with her political views.

The atmosphere of fear and the culture of remaining silent in public is so contagious that even people who express some form of conscious critique in their art may distance themselves from their own work under public pressure. This happened to another person I interviewed, who cited an expression of Boris Nemtsov’s (a liberal politcian who was assassinated in 2015) in his project that roughly translates to Putin is a nutjob. Right after the project’s first public showing at a festival, the artist in question decided to remove the phrase under pressure by the people surrounding him. He explained that he was afraid of being ostracized; he is a part of this society and wishes to continue being a part of it. When it became evident that the audience did not appreciate this gesture in his work, he felt as if his very presence was poisoning the atmosphere around him. People made it clear that problems may arise because of him, that they could lose funding, or be dragged into a public scandal.

This feeling was intolerable, because this artist wanted to work and be friends with those people; he wanted to continue to live in that country. A single digression — a single unwelcome phrase — could have cost him his career and made him a persona non-grata. Aware of this, he altered his project, even though nobody threatened him to change it. The festival’s organizers simply asked him not to mention the project’s connection to this festival in any of its future displays if he would not change anything, because he was told that would betray them all. For him, the entire situation came as a shock, as he could not have imagined that people who were liberals in private conversions would go to such lengths to avoid potential problems.

These events call to mind the Russian art community’s reaction to a recent interview given by the actor Aleksei Serebryakov in which he spoke out critically about the national identity of modern-day Russia. Within just three days of the interview, almost 300 articles about it were published across different Russian media outlets (Zelensky, 2018). Almost all of these included commentaries on the matter from famous cultural figures who vehemently disagreed with the way Serebryakov characterized Russia. A common theme in their statements was that he should not have the right to work in Russia anymore and be stripped of his citizenship (Zelensky, 2018). Everybody in the art community was quick to bandwagon behind the hostility against Serebryakov, possibly to ensure that it did not appear as if they supported him. The Russian art world demanded an apology, but Aleksei refused and stood by his words (Lushnikova, 2018). The actor had already emigrated to Canada together with his family in 2012, six years prior to this interview (“Alexei Serebryakov took”, 2012).

This kind of censorship is not a government-sanctioned reprimand but pressure exerted by society itself against people it perceives to be harmful or whose attitudes do not fall in line with theirs. As if delivering a vaccine to the Russian art world, the government has in the past punished specific artists, marking these as dangers to society. In time, the art community learns to autonomously banish artists who vocally oppose the regime, because these are now viewed as dangerous agents.

Even if you did not suspect that you were doing something wrong or should censor yourself, society will notify you of your misdoings and infect you with its fears. Since everyone surrounding you is afraid, you might think to heed to the concerns of the majority. This is akin to mass paranoia, whereby nobody is quite sure of what is happening or what kind of behavior might elicit punishment. Fears multiply and assume grotesque shapes. It is a paranoia that is almost impossible to withstand, as it pits individuals against society, an entity that is less prone to change. The more likely outcome is that it will simply reject you.

To censor oneself or not is always is a dilemma that only the individual can resolve, and the reasoning behind the choice they make will differ from person to person. It can be a matter of coveting success, the desire to avoid social or professional isolation, fear of the harsh criticism that awaits, reluctance to engage in politics, or even wanting to maintain a stable relationship with one’s family members. Important is that this type of censorship disorients and paralyzes us. We get lost in our doubts. Instead of working, we seek out how to avoid offending anyone or endlessly ruminate on the possible ramifications of our actions. As a result, we do not work at all or at half our capacity, all while anxiously awaiting our fate.

I am not proud of the fact that I am afraid to speak up about certain subjects even though I am far away from my home country. To me, it has been a significant leap forward to have simply addressed and organized these thoughts for myself. In discussing them, it is as if I switched on the lights in a room long kept dark and the shadows there have suddenly become less frightening. The simplest way of fighting self-censorship is not to shout from the roofs that society is censoring itself but to begin by being honest with oneself. Figuring out the answer to why you might self-censor can be the first step towards freeing yourself from its grasp, like it was for me.

The atmosphere of censorship changes not only artists but the entirety of the art world. Society becomes more aggressive toward those who are critical of the political system, societal standards, or the status quo. In so doing, self-censorship and fear are ingrained deeper and deeper. Someone may be afraid of being cast out from their profession, another may be afraid for their wellbeing, while someone else simply wants to avoid getting themselves or others in trouble. The result of this process in Russia has been that the people in power are criticized mainly in private conservations, rarely in public or in a work of art — a very painful and oppressive silence.

Conclusion

Conclusion

The focus of this thesis was on the formation of self-censored society in contemporary Russia, and the research question at the center of it sought to answer how the Russian government’s policy, from 2012 onwards, influenced artistic life in Russia and contributed to artists’ perceived need to self-censor their work.

The history of censorship in Russia runs deep. During Stalin’s reign of the USSR, artists came to be treated as instruments that served the authorities’ interests. Their task was not to express their own ideas but to meticulously adhere to the whims of officials. Art needed to instill confidence in the greatness of the country and optimism in its future, glorify the achievements of the government, and be simple enough to be understood by the general public. The foundation for this era’s censorship was repression and artists’ total dependence on the government. This policy achieved its goal, and, for a long time, art in the USSR only had one style: socialist realism.

Nowadays, the copious amount of information transferred over the Internet and the demands of modern economies make it infeasible to monitor and control the constant stream of information running through a country. Russian authorities have been unable to resort to the methods of censorship used by Stalin’s officials due to this. Until 2012, authorities did not pay much attention to contemporary artists, but their involvement in several large protests ahead of the 2012 presidential elections changed the government’s stance. That year, Pussy Riot also held a performance in Moscow at the Cathedral of Christ the Savior in reaction to Putin seeking out a third term as president and the Russian-Orthodox Church’s support for his reelection. Their performance attracted an incredibly harsh reaction from the government and signified an apparent shift in its policy towards art.

Looking back, 2012 was the beginning of a new form of censorship in Russia. Its goal was not secure total control of the art community but to create a society that, instead, censored itself autonomously. For an artist, self-censorship can range from refusing to worth with certain (controversial) subjects, avoiding publicizing a project that is perhaps already completed, to adapting a project in response to or in anticipation of societal pressure. This phenomenon can be evoked through a multitude of mechanisms, including aggression and intimidation. Officials used Pussy Riot’s performance as a way of legitimizing aggression directed against undesirable artists. They also revised, expanded, and strengthened the law protecting the feelings of the religious community. Since then, this law has been used against many critics of the political or religious establishment.

It was during this period that the narrative of contemporary artists being opponents of traditional values and the national culture of Russia began to take root. These artists were being portrayed as the internal enemies of the state, which helped to consolidate society around a leader who showed he would protect the country’s and the citizens’ interests. Additionally, pro-Church and pro-government activist groups increasingly took it upon themselves to act as censors of art they perceived as offensive, immoral, or unwelcome. Through their attacks, they succeeded at closing numerous plays, performances, and exhibitions. Yet, these groups’ actions went unpunished, which could be seen as a tacit sign of approval on the part of authorities. On top of this, state-run media harassed artists who publicly spoke out against the government or Church. Tellingly, not everyone who acted out of line was reproached. Rather, a select number were singled out and made an example of.

The lack of clear-cut laws and the selectivity of punishments seeds uncertainty within the art community. Because people do not understand what is and is not allowed, they set their own far harsher restrictions in trying to safeguard themselves from the possible repercussions of their actions. At the same time, the government encourages adhering to its ideology through financial incentives—a classic carrot-and-stick approach.

Though seemingly innocuous, the minister of culture’s declaration that the government will only fund art of a very specific nature and content has far-reaching consequences. It highlights that the Russian government is only interested in stimulating and sponsoring art that is in service of its own interests and values. In a country where independently-financed art is few and far between, the effect is that the government has significant leverage over artists and art institutions, and it has a great degree of influence over what kind of art the public is exposed to. These government-approved artists are asked to educate their audience about conservative and patriotic values. In consequence, art in which there is no space for critical views on the country, its history, or its future is being actively stimulated and thus produced. This is evocative of a modernized vision on USSR-era censorship—today’s artists are tasked with goals very similar to those in the socialist realist paradigm under Stalin. The mechanisms employed by the Russian government to further its own objectives — exploiting financial dependencies, the marketization of art, and selectively punishing certain artists on the basis of unclear rules — are indicative of a calculated policy of self-censorship.

Over time these mechanisms have compelled artists to increasingly self-censor their work and opinions. The response to Serebryakov’s and Raikin’s remarks on the state of Russia and its art world has shown that the art community will denounce critics of the system as a means of self-preservation, even if this means casting out one of their own. As evidenced by the accounts of one of this thesis’ interviewees, this type of behavior is not exclusive to the upper echelons of the art world either—it has permeated the system from top to bottom.

The motivations that drive an artist or any other person to self-censor their work or statements are wholly individual. Its underlying factors can be multi-layered and even confusing to disentangle for the individual themselves, as explored in the final chapter of this thesis. The classic example is, of course, a fear of being punished by authorities, but one's internalized anxieties may extend far wider, too. Perhaps it is the desire to be successful, wanting to be able to work in your home country, or being afraid for your own safety and wellbeing or that of your family — any combination of these or other factors can potentially drive an artist to reconsider publishing an outspoken piece of art or even commenting on sensitive topics. Moreover, each country - not just Russia - has its own subjects that are likely to arouse dissatisfaction among specific subsections of society. While this is not an issue in and of itself, the proliferation and tabooization of such topics should act as societal warning lights. Why are we afraid to talk about such matters? Is it because we are too polite or too afraid? Raising these questions, whether it is in the public discourse or to oneself, will be key to preserving the independence of art and cultural development.
